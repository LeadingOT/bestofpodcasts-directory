# What is Transcription

**Transcription is the process of converting spoken language from audio or video recordings into written text.**

At its core, transcription is an act of translation—taking the ephemeral nature of speech and rendering it into a permanent, searchable, and scannable format. This is typically done either by a human transcriptionist who listens and types or by Automated Speech Recognition (ASR) software powered by artificial intelligence. The output can range from a verbatim record, capturing every utterance, filler word, and non-verbal sound, to a clean or "intelligent verbatim" transcript that omits distractions for readability while preserving meaning. The accuracy and style of the transcript depend heavily on the quality of the source audio and the method chosen.

Transcription matters because it unlocks the content trapped within audio and video files, dramatically increasing its accessibility and utility. For content creators, like podcasters and YouTubers, transcripts improve SEO, allowing search engines to index their spoken words, which drives discovery. They also make content accessible to the deaf and hard-of-hearing community and serve audiences who prefer reading to listening. In professional and academic settings, transcription creates searchable records of meetings, interviews, lectures, and legal proceedings, enabling easy reference and analysis.

Practical use cases are widespread. A journalist transcribes an interview to pull exact quotes accurately. A student transcribes a lecture to create study notes. A company uses transcription to document a board meeting for the official record. A podcaster provides a transcript alongside an episode so a listener can quickly find a mentioned resource. Furthermore, transcription is often the essential first step for creating subtitles, closed captions, and translated content.

**Related Terms:** Automated Speech Recognition (ASR), Closed Captioning, Subtitling, Verbatim Transcript.
