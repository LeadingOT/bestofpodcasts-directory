# What is Audiogram

**An audiogram is a graphical chart that records the results of a hearing test, plotting the softest sounds a person can hear across different frequencies (pitches).**

An audiogram is created during a hearing assessment, typically conducted by an audiologist. The vertical axis (Y-axis) represents the intensity or volume of sound, measured in decibels (dB), ranging from very soft (-10 dB) to very loud (120 dB). The horizontal axis (X-axis) represents frequency, measured in Hertz (Hz), ranging from low pitches (125 Hz, like a bass drum) to high pitches (8000 Hz, like a bird chirping). During the test, you listen for tones at each frequency, and the point at which you can just barely hear a tone is marked on the chart. These marks, usually marked with an 'O' for the right ear and an 'X' for the left ear, create a unique "hearing fingerprint" for each individual.

The resulting graph provides a precise visual snapshot of a person's hearing ability. A typical pattern for age-related hearing loss, for example, often shows a steeper drop in the high-frequency range, meaning softer, high-pitched sounds are harder to hear. An audiogram can diagnose the type (e.g., which part of the ear is affected), degree (mild, moderate, severe), and configuration (which frequencies are impacted) of hearing loss. It is the fundamental tool for determining the exact nature of a hearing impairment.

**Why it matters / Use cases:** The audiogram is the cornerstone of audiology. It is essential for diagnosing hearing loss, fitting and programming hearing aids or cochlear implants, monitoring hearing changes over time, and assessing fitness for certain jobs with hearing requirements (e.g., military, aviation). For podcast creators and audio engineers, understanding audiograms is also valuable. For instance, if a significant portion of your audience has age-related high-frequency loss, you might ensure your voice recording is clear in the mid-frequencies or consider using subtitles. An audiogram provides the objective data needed to make informed decisions about hearing health and audio content accessibility.

**Related terms:** **Decibel (dB)**, **Hertz (Hz)**, **Audiologist**, **Hearing Aid Fitting**, **Tympanometry**.
